# Workshop 3 Facilitator Guide (75 min)

**Title:** Data & Accounts — What’s Collected, What’s Remembered, and Why Defaults Matter  
**Theme:** Data is information; accounts help tools remember you; defaults shape privacy and behavior.  
**Audience:** Families with kids ~4–10  
**Format:** Parent + child required (no drop-off). Minimal screens. No open internet. No kid accounts.

---

## 1) Purpose & outcomes

### Big idea (kid-safe)
- **Data** is information.
- An **account** is a “name tag + memory” that lets a tool remember you.
- **Defaults** are the settings you start with.

Kid version:
> “Data is clues. Accounts help apps remember. Defaults are the starting rules.”

### What kids should be able to do by the end
- Give examples of **data** (name, birthday, location, watch history, favorites).
- Explain why an account exists: “So it can remember me.”
- Practice a simple rule: **“Ask a parent before making an account or entering personal info.”**

### What parents should be able to do by the end
- Name 4 common kinds of data tools collect:
  1) Identity/contact (name/email)
  2) Device/technical (device type)
  3) Behavior (what you watch/click)
  4) Location (approximate)
- Use the **3-step decision** for kid tools:
  1) What data does this collect?
  2) What does it do with that data (job)?
  3) What default should we change?
- Leave with a **Family Account Rules** card (simple, enforceable defaults).

---

## 2) Facilitator mindset

### Tone
- Calm, not paranoid.
- Avoid: “Big Tech is evil” rants.
- Aim for: “We choose what we share, and we use defaults intentionally.”

### Redirection script (when parents get anxious)
> “The goal isn’t to memorize every setting. The goal is to choose a few simple default rules your family can follow consistently.”

## 4) Safety / boundaries
- Kids stay with their adult.
- No sharing personal info during activities.
- Keep examples generic (no child full names/birthdays aloud).

---

## 5) Run of show (minute-by-minute)

### 0–5 min — Welcome + quick recap
Script:
> “Welcome back. Week 1: tools made by people. Week 2: systems and automation. Week 3: data and accounts—what tools remember, and how we choose what to share.”
> 
> “Kind, curious, no shaming. Parent + child together.”

Quick prompt:
- “Did anyone try one automation once?” Celebrate any win.

### 5–15 min — Teach: Data / Account / Default (kid-safe)
**Goal:** clear definitions.

Teach (2–3 min):
- Data = information (clues)
- Account = sign-in + memory
- Default = starting setting

Kid analogy (use one):
- **Library card:** lets the library remember what you checked out.
- **Name tag at a party:** helps people remember who you are.

Micro-script:
> “An account helps an app remember you—what you like, what you watched, your settings. That can be helpful… and it can also make it harder to stop.”

Call-and-response:
- “Data!” → “Information!”
- “Account!” → “Sign in + memory!”
- “Default!” → “Starting rule!”

Family prompt (3–4 min):
> “Name one tool that remembers you. What does it remember?”

Share 2 quick examples.

### 15–40 min — Activity 1: Keep / Share / Ask First sorting
**Goal:** families practice making privacy choices.

Set-up (1 min):
> “We’re going to sort data types into three buckets for your family: Keep private, Share sometimes, Ask first.”

Instructions (2 min):
- Spread the data cards.
- Sort into:
  - **Keep private** (almost never)
  - **Share** (okay with parent)
  - **Ask first** (always ask a parent)

Facilitator note:
- This isn’t about being perfect; it’s about **agreement**.

Work time (12–15 min):
- Walk around and ask:
  - “What’s the job of sharing this?”
  - “What could go wrong if it’s shared widely?”
  - “Is this needed for the job, or just extra?”

Mini-share (8–10 min):
- Ask 3 families:
  - “One card you put in ‘Keep private’ and why.”
  - Keep it short.

Kid-friendly anchor rule (say out loud):
> “If you’re not sure, you ask your adult.”

### 40–55 min — Activity 2: The ‘Account Machine’ (why apps ask for sign-in)
**Goal:** explain motivations without fear.

Draw on board:
**You → (Data) → Tool remembers → Suggestions / ads / easier login**

Script (5–6 min):
> “Apps ask for accounts because it helps them remember. Remembering can be good (saved progress). And it can be used to keep you watching or buying.”

Do 2 quick scenarios (ask the room):
1) “A game wants an account.”
   - What does it want to remember?
   - What’s the benefit?
   - What’s the risk?
2) “A video app wants an account.”
   - What does it remember?
   - What output does that create? (recommendations)

Family prompt (3–4 min):
> “Pick one app/tool in your house. What does the account help it remember?”

Share 2 examples.

### 55–70 min — Family deliverable: Family Account Rules
**Goal:** leave with defaults that prevent problems.

Introduce (1 min):
> “Now we’ll write the defaults that keep you from having to decide in the moment.”

Families fill out the handout:
1) **Account rule:** “Ask a parent before creating an account.”
2) **Personal info rule:** “Ask a parent before entering name/email/birthday/location.”
3) **Password rule:** “Passwords are parent-managed.”
4) **Purchase rule (optional):** “No purchases without a parent.”
5) **Device rule (optional):** “Devices park at night in a public spot.”

Make it age-flexible:
- For younger kids: keep to 2–3 rules.
- For older kids: add “Why this rule exists.”

Share-out (optional, 2 min):
- Ask for one rule a family is excited to try.

### 70–75 min — Close + Family Move
Script:
> “Family Move: choose ONE rule from your card and use it once this week.”
> 
> “Next week: interfaces and habits—how design choices shape behavior, and how to build boundaries that stick.”

---

## 6) Common scenarios & handling

### A) Parent: “So should we never make accounts?”
> “Not necessarily. Accounts can be useful. The goal is: accounts on purpose, with defaults you choose.”

### B) Kid: “Why can’t I just put my birthday?”
> “Because that’s personal info. In our family, personal info is ‘ask first.’”

### C) Parent: “This feels overwhelming.”
> “Totally normal. You only need a few defaults. Consistency beats complexity.”

## Appendix A — Suggested FAQ prompts (if you need them)

- “Does the tool need this info to do the job?”
- “Could we do the job without an account?”
- “What’s the default? Would we change it?”
- “If we share this, who else might see it?”

## Appendix B — Suggested Family Account Rules (menu)

- Ask before new accounts
- Ask before personal info
- Parent-managed passwords
- No purchases without parent
- No devices in bedrooms
- Turn off non-essential notifications
