# Workshop 4 Facilitator Guide (75 min)

**Title:** Interfaces & Habits — Design Shapes Behavior (and We Can Choose Boundaries)  
**Theme:** Interfaces are “handles” that guide behavior. Habits + environment beat willpower.  
**Audience:** Families with kids ~4–10  
**Format:** Parent + child required (no drop-off). Minimal screens. No open internet. No kid accounts.

---

## 1) Purpose & outcomes

### Big idea (kid-safe)
- An **interface** is the part you touch: buttons, menus, autoplay, “up next.”
- Interfaces are designed to guide what you do next.
- Families can choose **boundaries** that make good habits easier.

Kid version:
> “Interfaces are the handles. Handles can pull you. We can choose our rules.”

### What kids should be able to do by the end
- Identify interface “handles” that pull attention (autoplay, bright buttons, streaks).
- Practice a simple stop skill: **Pause → Ask → Choose**.
- Help build one family boundary they agree with.

### What parents should be able to do by the end
- Recognize common interface patterns that increase usage:
  - autoplay, recommendations, infinite scroll, streaks, notifications
- Choose a small set of “default” boundaries that reduce fights.
- Leave with a **Family Tech Plan** (boundaries + routines + enforcement script).

---

## 2) Facilitator mindset

### Tone
- Empowering and non-judgmental.
- Avoid: “if you loved your kid you’d never…”
- Emphasize: boundaries are about **protecting what you value**.

### Redirection script (when parents debate ideology)
> “Different families choose different boundaries. Our goal is a plan that fits your values and your real life.”

## 5) Run of show (minute-by-minute)

### 0–5 min — Welcome + recap
Script:
> “Final week. Week 1: tools and jobs. Week 2: systems and automation. Week 3: data and accounts. Today: interfaces and habits—how design pulls attention, and how families set boundaries that stick.”

Quick win prompt:
- “Did anyone use one account rule or ask-first rule once?”

### 5–15 min — Teach: Interfaces are handles
**Goal:** kids can name what an interface is.

Teach (2–3 min):
- Interface = the part you touch (buttons/menus/scroll)
- Interfaces guide the next action

Show on board (no devices):
- Draw a fake app screen with:
  - Big play button
  - Up next countdown
  - Scroll feed
  - Notification bell

Ask kids:
- “What do you think this screen wants you to do?”

Introduce the stop skill:
- **Pause → Ask → Choose**

Call-and-response:
- “Pause!” (kids freeze)
- “Ask!” (kids point to adult)
- “Choose!” (kids pick left/right)

### 15–35 min — Activity 1: Pattern spotting + “What’s the next button?”
**Goal:** families learn to notice patterns.

Instructions (2 min):
> “Sort these interface cards into two piles: ‘Helps me’ and ‘Pulls me’—or put in both and explain the job.”

Work time (8–10 min):
- Walk around asking:
  - “What job does this do?”
  - “When does it pull too hard?”

Mini-share (8–10 min):
- Ask 3 families to share one pattern they think is the biggest ‘pull.’

Facilitator point:
> “When you know the pattern, you can choose a boundary instead of relying on willpower.”

### 35–55 min — Activity 2: Boundary design sprint (menu + choose 3)
**Goal:** families choose boundaries they will actually follow.

Set-up (1 min):
> “Now we’ll pick a few default boundaries. Not 20. Just a few that reduce fights.”

Boundary categories (write on board):
- **Time** (when)
- **Place** (where)
- **Content** (what)
- **People** (who with)
- **Friction** (what we turn off)

Give families the Boundary Menu handout and ask them to choose:
- **1 Time boundary**
- **1 Place boundary**
- **1 Friction boundary** (turn off a pull)

Examples you can read aloud:
- Time: “Weekdays: no personal screens before school.”
- Place: “No devices in bedrooms.”
- Friction: “Autoplay off; notifications off after 7pm.”

Work time (10–12 min):
- Coach for clarity and realism.

Quick share (2–3 min):
- 2 families share their favorite boundary.

### 55–65 min — Family deliverable: Family Tech Plan (final)
**Goal:** one-page plan families can use.

Families complete:
1) Our family uses tech for (3 values)
2) Our defaults (the 3 boundaries they picked)
3) Our “Pause → Ask → Choose” rule for kids
4) Our enforcement script (one sentence)

Enforcement scripts (give options):
- “I’m not mad. The rule is the rule. We can try again tomorrow.”
- “Screens are done. Do you want to turn it off, or should I?”
- “We can be upset and still follow the plan.”

Coach parents to avoid long lectures.

### 65–73 min — Capstone: The AI Reveal (kid-safe)
**Goal:** connect Weeks 1–4 into a clear, non-mystical definition of AI, and give 2–3 safe family rules.

#### 1-minute bridge (say this)
> “You’ve actually learned the building blocks of AI already. AI is not magic. It’s tools made by people (Week 1), running systems (Week 2), trained on data (Week 3), delivered through an interface (Week 4).”

Write on the board:
- **AI = patterns learned from lots of examples (data) + rules + outputs**

#### Option A (recommended): “Human AI” paper demo (no screens) — 5 minutes
**Setup:** pick 2 volunteers (older kids or two parents) to be “the model.”

- Give them a sheet of 8–10 example pairs (your “training data”). Example set:
  - Input: “It’s bedtime.” → Output: “Brush teeth.”
  - Input: “I’m bored.” → Output: “Pick a puzzle.”
  - Input: “I want YouTube.” → Output: “Ask a parent.”
  - Input: “I’m hungry.” → Output: “Get a snack.”
  - Input: “Can I download an app?” → Output: “Ask first.”
  - Input: “I’m frustrated.” → Output: “Take a break.”
  - Input: “What’s the job?” → Output: “To help us.”
  - Input: “Pause!” → Output: “Ask! Choose!”

**Run it:**
1) “Training” (30 seconds): volunteers read the pairs.
2) “Testing” (2 minutes): families call out NEW inputs that are similar.
   - Volunteers respond with the closest matching output.
3) “Debrief” (2 minutes):
   - “Did the model understand? Or was it matching patterns?”
   - “What happens if training examples are weird or wrong?” (bias)

**Key line:**
> “AI doesn’t have wisdom. It has pattern power.”

#### Option B (backup): 60-second autocomplete demo (presenter-controlled)
If you use a screen: keep it short.
- Type a prompt and let it complete.
- Say: “It predicts what comes next based on patterns it learned.”

#### Parent takeaways (30–60 seconds)
- AI outputs can be useful **and** wrong.
- The training examples matter (bias).
- Don’t share personal info into AI tools.

#### Family AI rules (write these and have them circle 1–2)
- **Ask first** before using AI/chatbots.
- **No personal info** (full name, birthday, school, address).
- **Treat answers as suggestions**; double-check with an adult.

### 73–75 min — Close + next steps
Script:
> “Your Family Move: pick ONE boundary and run it for one week—no changes mid-week. Then adjust.”
> 
> “If you want help customizing your family plan, reply to hi@chipgpt.biz.”

Celebration:
- Quick applause for parents and kids.

## 6) Common scenarios & handling

### A) Parent: “My kid melts down when screens end.”
> “That’s common. Two tools: more friction (turn off autoplay) and a simple script you repeat calmly. Consistency beats intensity.”

### B) Kid: “But it’s not fair!”
> “Fair means everyone follows the same family plan. You can be mad and still follow the plan.”

### C) Parents disagree
> “Pick the smallest shared boundary you both can enforce consistently. Start there.”

## Appendix A — Boundary Menu (starter list)

**Time**
- No personal screens before school
- Screens after chores/homework
- One show then stop (use a timer)

**Place**
- No devices in bedrooms
- Devices parked during meals
- Charging station in kitchen at night

**Content**
- YouTube only with a parent
- No new apps without a parent

**People**
- Co-watch for new shows
- Friends don’t set the rules

**Friction (turn off pulls)**
- Autoplay off
- Notifications off after 7pm
- Remove app icons from home screen

## Appendix B — “Pause → Ask → Choose” kid prompts

- Pause: “Stop hands.”
- Ask: “Is this okay?”
- Choose: “What’s our family plan?”
